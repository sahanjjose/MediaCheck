Understading The Fundamentals

The key distinguishing principles between a democracy and authoritarian rule lie in the source of power and the nature of governance. In a democracy, power is ultimately vested in the people, who either directly or through elected representatives participate in decision-making processes. Democracies uphold principles such as popular sovereignty, political equality, rule of law, majority rule with minority rights, freedom of expression, political pluralism, and free and fair elections. On the other hand, authoritarian rule is characterized by the concentration of power in the hands of a single leader or a small group, with limited or no input from the general population. Authoritarian regimes often suppress political dissent, restrict freedoms, and do not uphold the rule of law or respect minority rights. The key distinction is that democracies prioritize the rights and participation of the people, while authoritarian regimes prioritize centralized control and often use coercion to maintain power.

Examining our democratic nations under the parameters above, recent events highlight significant challenges to democratic principles. The January 6 United States Capitol attack was an attempt to overturn the popular sovereignty of the 2020 presidential election by supporters of then-President Donald Trump. “The rise of Narendra Modi and right-wing Hindu nationalism in India” (Mihelj and Jiménez-Martínez 331-346) is an attack on majority rule with minority rights. Be it Modi’s “Selfie Nationalism” (Rao 166-183) or Trump’s “ability to utterly sop up the media coverage 140 characters at a time” (Wimberly 179-199) both have mastered mass communication through their clever use of digital media space as a form of public relations atleast until they had not achieved the power to control mainstream media or diminish their relevance.
